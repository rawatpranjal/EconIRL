Thank you for the question about IQ-Learn. You are right that the paper is hard to penetrate. The good news is that the algorithm is doing something very familiar once you see through the notation. I have attached a short derivation note (8 pages) that shows everything step by step with explicit equations, a numerical example, and a comparison with NFXP. The note uses the notation from our survey throughout. Below I try to answer each of your questions directly.

Q: "I find the paper that explains the estimator to be impenetrable and unintuitive... Do you have a good intuitive explanation of what this algorithm is doing?"
A: IQ-Learn with the chi-squared regularizer is penalized conditional log-likelihood estimation. The first term is the standard multinomial logit likelihood that fits expert actions through a softmax policy. The second term is a squared penalty on the Bellman-implied reward. The Q-function is the single free parameter. It determines both the policy (via softmax) and the implied reward (via the inverse Bellman operator, r = Q - beta E[V]). No inner Bellman loop is needed because V is an explicit closed-form function of Q through the logsumexp. I derive this equivalence in the attached note using only two algebraic substitutions into the IQ-Learn objective, and verify every step numerically on a small MDP.

Q: "What regularizer did you use in your implementation and is there a more intuitive explanation of what this algorithm is doing?"
A: Chi-squared with alpha = 1. This gives a quadratic penalty on the implied reward, which is why the authors find it "readily interpretable." Jensen-Shannon gives a non-quadratic penalty that does not correspond to a familiar norm. The choice of regularizer determines which member of the equivalence class of observationally equivalent rewards is returned. Chi-squared picks the one with smallest squared reward at expert-visited states.

Q: "I don't know why it needs to improve the policy in Algorithm 1 when the optimal policy given Q is just the softmax."
A: You are exactly right. In the finite discrete case, the policy improvement step is unnecessary. The optimal policy given Q is just the softmax, computed in closed form. The policy improvement loop appears in the paper only because it targets continuous action spaces where one cannot enumerate all actions to compute the softmax. In our DDC setting it does not appear at all. Our implementation has no policy improvement loop.

Q: "I noticed from the timing that it is one of the fastest methods."
A: There is no nested fixed-point computation. NFXP solves Q = Lambda(Q) in an inner loop for every evaluation of the likelihood. IQ-Learn parameterizes Q directly and computes V = sigma logsumexp(Q/sigma) in closed form. Each objective evaluation is a single forward pass through these expressions. On the 5-state bus engine problem it converges in under a tenth of a second.

Q: "It seems to just maximize directly over reward functions without mentioning the identification problem and multiplicity of solutions that would result from this, except for using 'regularization' but this seems rather ad hoc."
A: The identification problem is handled by the regularizer, not resolved by it. Many rewards rationalize the same observed behavior. Any reward of the form r'(s,a) = r(s,a) + h(s) - beta sum h(s') p(s'|s,a) for an arbitrary function h yields the same optimal policy. The chi-squared penalty selects from this equivalence class by favoring the reward with smallest squared magnitude at expert-visited states. This is analogous to how ridge regression picks the minimum-norm solution when the design matrix is rank deficient. Different divergences pick different members of the equivalence class, which is why the authors note that some choices lead to less interpretable reward estimates.

Q: "There is a statement in their paper that seems to admit that some regularizer choices lead to uninterpretable reward function estimates... Compared to chi-squared, we find other f-divergences like Jensen-Shannon result in similar performances but are not as readily interpretable."
A: The chi-squared divergence gives a quadratic (L2) penalty on the implied reward. This selects the minimum-norm reward, which is easy to interpret. The Jensen-Shannon divergence gives a non-quadratic penalty that does not correspond to a standard norm. The resulting reward is a different member of the equivalence class with no clean interpretation as a "smallest" reward in any familiar sense. The choice of divergence does not affect the fit to expert actions. It only affects which observationally equivalent reward is selected.

Q: "We did not cover it in our article and partly that is my fault since I did not feel I could succinctly describe what IQ is doing and the motivation for it."
A: A possible paragraph for the article: "In the finite discrete offline setting with known transitions, the IQ-Learn chi-squared objective (Garg et al., 2021) can be rewritten as a penalized conditional log-likelihood for the softmax policy induced by Q. The first term is the multinomial logit conditional log-likelihood with Q-values as choice-specific utilities. The second term is an L2 penalty on the Bellman-implied reward at expert-visited states. Because the smoothed value function is an explicit function of Q, no inner Bellman solve is needed, making IQ-Learn computationally fast relative to NFXP. The penalty selects from the equivalence class of observationally equivalent rewards by favoring the solution with smallest implied reward."
